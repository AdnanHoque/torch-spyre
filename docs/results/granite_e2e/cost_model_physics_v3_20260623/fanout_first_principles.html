Fanout: why N‑split hurts and M‑split doesn't

The cost model charges fanout mostly to the N‑split. This isn't about which tensor is bigger — it's about which operand has to reach many cores at the same time on the hot path. In a weight‑stationary array that operand is the activation stream, and N‑split duplicates it while M‑split partitions it.

This note unpacks one question: why does fanout cost mostly depend on n_split? It builds the intuition from toy pictures, then connects it to why the planner treats a shared‑weight projection and a true‑BMM attention op differently. It's the conceptual companion to the v3 cost-model explainer — this one is all intuition and toy diagrams.

1 · The setup: weight-stationary matmul

Every tile of work is a small matmul:

A[M, K]  @  W[K, N]  →  C[M, N]
        # A = activations (tokens)   W = the right-hand operand   C = output

The hardware runs this weight‑stationary. Three steps, and the order is the whole story:

Hold onto this The weight is the resident operand (loaded once, reused). The activation is the streaming operand (needed every beat). That asymmetry is the reason N‑split and M‑split cost different amounts.

2 · A 2D split is just a grid of cores

A split like {m:2, n:4} means: lay the cores out as a 2×4 grid. Each core computes one tile of the output C — the rows for its M‑group crossed with the columns for its N‑group.

Toy shape: M=8, K=4, N=8, split {m:2, n:4} → 2×4 = 8 cores.

So for your two examples, with {m, n, k}:

Your "case 1 vs case 2" reading is directionally right: in {m:8,n:4} the weight is shared by 8 cores and the activation by 4; in {m:4,n:8} it flips. Which one the planner prefers depends on what the RHS is — we get to that in §6.

3 · Who shares what: N‑split shares activations, M‑split shares weights

Take the same grid and ask, for each direction, "which operand is identical across these cores?"

Your statement, confirmed "for n‑split → activations are shared by n cores; for m‑split → weights are shared by m cores" — yes, exactly. The next section is the subtle part: those two "sharings" do not cost the same.

4 · "But weights are bigger — isn't broadcasting activations cheaper?"

This is the natural objection, and it's the crux. The answer is that fanout cost is not about total bytes. It's about which operand has to be delivered to many cores at the same moment, on the path that runs every beat.

The one-sentence answer It's cheaper to keep one big weight tile resident and reuse it than to fan a small activation stream out to many consumers, because the weight's reuse is amortized and off the hot path, while the activation's fanout lands on the hot path, every beat. "Which is bigger" never enters into it.

6 · Why shared-weight and true-BMM disagree about N‑split

Everything so far is mechanics that apply to any matmul. What makes a projection and an attention BMM behave differently is the meaning of the right-hand operand.

That distinction is exactly one line in the cost model:

fanout_split = max(m, n) if shared_weight else n
        # shared-weight projection: both m and n fan operands out
        # true BMM (attention):  only n fans the streaming activation
The asymmetry in one breath Shared‑weight: split N to divide the big stationary weight — both directions cost fanout, and N‑split is often good. True‑BMM: there's no global weight to divide, so N‑split only fans out the streaming activation — reach for batch/head + modest M instead.

7 · The payoff: decode attention @ V

This is where it all cashes out. Decode has tiny M (one or a few new tokens), so the array is starving for rows. The old planner reached for "use all 32 cores by splitting M" — which leaves each core almost nothing to do. The fix isn't more N‑split; it's head parallelism.

8 · Cheat sheet

N‑split

duplicates the activation stream

Same A rows feed many output-column workers at once → multicast/replay on the hot path. The expensive direction.

M‑split

partitions the activation rows

Different rows to different cores; the stationary weight is reused (free). Clean — until the per‑core tile gets too short to feed the array.

B / head split

partitions independent problems

Different heads are separate matmuls. The cleanest parallelism of all — no shared operand to fan out.

operandrole in weight-stationarywhat its fanout costs
Weight / RHSstationary — loaded once, heldcheap: reuse is amortized, off the hot path (this is M‑split's "sharing")
Activation / LHSstreaming — needed every beatexpensive: fanout is multicast/replay on the hot path (this is N‑split's "sharing")
One-line version Shared‑weight projection: split N to divide the big stationary weight (and keep enough M to feed the array).
True‑BMM attention: split batch/head and M to avoid tiny row tiles; don't over-split N — it just fans the streaming activation out to more workers.

And the answer to the question we started with: fanout depends mostly on n_split because n_split is the one that asks the streaming operand — the activation, the thing already on the critical path every beat — to be in many places at once. m_split only asks the resident operand to be reused, which is exactly what a weight‑stationary array is built to do for free.
