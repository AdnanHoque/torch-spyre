What is actually happening in the MLP benchmark on Spyre

A first-principles reading of the mlp micro-benchmark in the spyre-perf-suite — what the op is, how it lowers onto the AIU 1.0 array, what each timing number means, and why the decode numbers look the way they do. Written to answer four questions: what's in the MLP, why are the numbers what they are, is 5–8 ms/MLP anomalous (→ ~200 ms/token?), and is that wall, spyre, or kernel time — plus a fifth (§10): why the wide-N [4096, 12800] matmul runs ~20% over sendnn, and whether that is a split-choice bug we can fix or a kernel/bandwidth wall.

1. The questions

The benchmark report shows, per MLP call, kernel times in the 2–8 ms range and a device-time (spyre_ms) around 20–35 ms. Three questions follow:

  1. What is the "mlp" op? One FFN block, or a whole layer, or a whole model?
  2. Is 5–8 ms per MLP anomalous? Multiplied by a layer count it implies something like ~200 ms/token, which would be far too slow for a decode step.
  3. Which clock is that? Wall-clock, total device time (spyre_ms), or just the matmul kernel time?
  4. And the wide-N matmul (§10): a separate flag — the standalone [1,512,4096]@[4096,12800] matmul (the MLP's front projection, measured alone) runs ~20% slower than sendnn. Is that the planner picking a bad split, or a kernel/bandwidth wall no split can beat?

The short answers, derived below: the op is a single SwiGLU FFN (two/three matmuls); the decode kernel time is not anomalous in the sense of "a bug," it is memory-bound weight streaming — at one token per step the 8×8 systolic array has almost no math to do (PT-Util ~0.15%), so the ~2.8 ms it spends is the time to stream the FFN's 402.7 MB of weights in from memory at the bus's real read speed, with essentially zero fixed overhead; and the 200 ms/token figure comes from multiplying an isolated, per-call measurement by a layer count, which double-charges a per-call weight reload (~33 ms of Memset/Memcpy) that amortizes away once weights stay resident in a real autoregressive model.

2. The op: a SwiGLU FFN

Straight from the benchmark's op definition (benchmark.py, the mlp function):

def mlp(x, gate, up, down):
    gate_out   = x @ gate              # [M, 4096] @ [4096, 16384]
    up_out     = x @ up                # [M, 4096] @ [4096, 16384]
    swiglu_out = up_out * silu(gate_out)   # elementwise on [M, 16384]
    return swiglu_out @ down           # [M, 16384] @ [16384, 4096]

The tensor builder fixes the shapes: embedding dimension emb = 4096, and the intermediate ("hidden") dimension is 4 * emb = 16384. So the block is:

stepshapeMACs (per token, M=1)
gate = x@Wg[M,4096]@[4096,16384]67.1M
up = x@Wu[M,4096]@[4096,16384]67.1M
h = silu(gate)*upelementwise [M,16384]~0 (pointwise)
out = h@Wd[M,16384]@[16384,4096]67.1M
total—201.3M

This is the canonical Llama/Qwen-style SwiGLU MLP: two parallel "front" projections into a 4× wider space, a SiLU gate times the up-projection, then one "down" projection back to emb. It is one FFN block — not a layer (no attention, no norms), not a model (no layer stack). Everything below is the cost of that single block, run in isolation.

Two shapes are benchmarked:

  • Prefill: M = 512 tokens at once (a 512-token sequence). Total work ≈ 1.03×1011 MACs.
  • Decode: one token per step. The profiled decode case is a 4-token batch [4, 1, 4096] (4 independent sequences, one token each), i.e. M = 1 per sequence. Total work ≈ 8.05×108 MACs — about 128× less than prefill.

Hold onto that 128×: the decode kernel does ~128× less arithmetic, yet (we'll see) it takes about the same wall time as prefill. That gap is the whole story.

3. How it lowers on Spyre

The two stacks fuse the FFN differently, which matters when you read the per-kernel table.

torch-spyre (Inductor) splits the block into two compute kernels plus a Memset:

  • sdsc_fused_bmm_mul_silu_0 — the front half: gate-mm + up-mm + SiLU + the elementwise multiply, all fused into one SDSC kernel that emits the [M, 16384] intermediate.
  • sdsc_fused_bmm_1 — the down projection: [M,16384]@[16384,4096].
  • a Memset to zero an accumulator (sub-millisecond).

sendnn (the production backend) fuses the whole FFN into a single bmm kernel, plus host↔device Memcpy (HtoD / DtoH) and a Memset. So when comparing, remember: torch-spyre's "front + down" is two rows that you sum; sendnn's "bmm" is one row that already contains everything.

4. The three metrics, defined

The benchmark reports several clocks (see run_benchmark / write_stats in benchmark.py). Three matter, and conflating them is the source of the 200 ms/token confusion:

metricwhat it measuresincludes
kernel_ms Sum of the compute SDSC kernel durations (the bmm/sdsc_fused_* rows), excluding Memset, Memcpy, and host launch/dispatch (those are wall time). This is the actual PT-array work. matmul + fused pointwise on-device
PT-Util (%) How full the 8×8 systolic array is during the kernel — the fraction of the 64 multiply-accumulate cells doing useful work, averaged over the kernel. The smoking gun. array occupancy, not a time
spyre_ms Total device time for the call, summed over all device events: kernels plus per-call stickify, HBM staging, Memset, and (sendnn) host↔device Memcpy. everything the device did this call

There is also wall_clock_ms (Python perf_counter around the call, including the .to(device) / .cpu() transfers and dispatch — hundreds of ms here, dominated by host overhead and the per-call H2D/D2H of weights) and cpu_ms (self CPU time). Those are not the interesting signal for "is the accelerator slow."

Which clock is Jamie's table?
Jamie's per-kernel profile reports kernel_ms — "bmm kernel time only", broken down per kernel with PT-Util. It is not wall-clock and not spyre_ms. That distinction is the crux of §8.

5. The data: prefill vs decode

We have two independent measurements of the same benchmark, taken on different setups. We keep both and show them side by side — not to pick a winner, but because they disagree in an instructive way that §7–§8 and §14 explain. Throughout this doc:

  • Dataset 1 — "Jamie's profile." The per-kernel profile the doc was originally built around. In its decode run, every call ships fresh weights and activations between the host computer and the device, so its decode kernel rows are large.
  • Dataset 2 — "our canonical." A later run measured two ways: a persistent-weight configuration (the model's weights are loaded onto the device once and left there, the way a real model runs), and a production configuration (weights reloaded every call, like Dataset 1). The two agree once you account for that reloading — that is the bridge that resolves the disagreement (§8).

Read the per-kernel rows, not the "Total"
The profiler screenshot also has a "Total" row, but it is an aggregate / per-iteration figure that does not equal the sum of the kernel durations. Don't over-rely on it. The signal is the per-kernel duration + PT-Util pair.

The arresting fact: the Dataset 1 decode front kernel (7.979 ms) is essentially the same as the prefill front kernel (7.109 ms) — despite doing 128× fewer tokens of work. If the kernel were compute-bound, decode would be ~128× faster. It isn't. But notice that Dataset 2's persistent decode is much smaller (2.8 ms total), which is the first hint that most of Dataset 1's decode number is not the matmul at all — it is weight-reload memory traffic. The next sections pull these apart: §6–7 explain why the array does almost no math at decode, and why what is left is memory streaming, not idle overhead; §8 reconciles the 12 ms and the 2.8 ms.

6. The PT-Util collapse

PT-Util goes from ~20–30% at prefill to ~0.15% at decode. That is a ~140× drop in array occupancy — the systolic array is essentially idle during the decode kernel.

First, the plain-language meaning of this number. The matmul engine is an 8×8 systolic array — think of it as a small assembly line of 64 worker cells that only runs at full speed when tokens keep flowing in to keep every cell fed. Each pass feeds it 8 rows of M (_PT_ROWS = 8 in work_division.py), and it reaches full pipeline fill at roughly _TARGET_PT_PASSES = 8 passes — about 64 rows of M. The number of rows you feed it is M:

  • Prefill M = 512 → ~8 full passes per core (after an m-split) → the assembly line is kept fed; the array fills.
  • Decode M = 1 → one row. A single token enters an 8×8 array, so at most ~1 of the array's rows is active and the other ~63 cells do nothing — there is simply almost no arithmetic to do for a single token.
Low PT-Util means "no math to do," not "wasting time on overhead"
It is tempting to read "0.15% utilization" as "the array is busy with bookkeeping." That is the wrong picture, and the rest of this doc corrects it. PT-Util is low at decode for a simpler reason: a single token contains almost no arithmetic. There is nothing to fill the array with. The time the kernel spends is not idle bookkeeping — it is spent streaming the model's weights in from memory so the few multiplies that do happen have something to multiply against. In other words, decode is memory-bound (the chip finishes its trickle of math far faster than memory can deliver the weights, so it waits on memory), not overhead-bound. §7 makes this quantitative; §14 measures the actual memory speed.

7. Why decode is memory-bound weight streaming

The array does almost no arithmetic at decode (§6). So where do the milliseconds go? The earlier reading was "fixed overhead." A direct measurement (the canonical Dataset 2, and the bandwidth probe of §14) says something sharper and more hopeful: the time is spent streaming the model's weights in from memory. Decode is memory-bound weight streaming, and it runs near the memory ceiling with almost no fixed overhead at all.

7.1 The two jobs, and which one wins

Every matmul has two jobs: (1) fetch the weights from the device's main memory (HBM), and (2) do the math (multiply the token by the weights). Picture a highway with a fixed number of lanes carrying weights from a warehouse to the assembly line. At decode:

  • The math is a trickle — one token's worth, ~16 µs if the array were perfectly fed.
  • The weight fetch is the whole job — the FFN's weight matrices total 402.7 MB, and every one of those bytes must cross the highway, no matter how few tokens you are processing. A single token reuses each weight exactly once.

So the kernel finishes its trickle of math almost instantly and then spends the rest of its time waiting for weights to arrive over the highway. That is what "memory-bound" means: the chip is faster at the math than memory is at delivering the weights, so the clock is set by memory.

7.2 The decode kernel runs at the memory ceiling, not on overhead

Here is the measurement that settles it. The canonical persistent-weight decode kernel (Dataset 2) is 2.830 ms for torch-spyre and 2.795 ms for sendnn. Now divide the weight bytes by that time:

2.8 ms is exactly the time to stream 402.7 MB of weights
402.7 MB / 2.83 ms ≈ 142 GB/s (torch-spyre) and 402.7 MB / 2.80 ms ≈ 144 GB/s (sendnn). §14 measures the device's real read-dominated streaming ceiling at ~140–146 GB/s — so the decode kernel is running right at the bus's read speed. There is essentially no fixed overhead left over: the 2.8 ms is weight streaming, full stop. Both stacks land within ~1% of each other and of the ceiling, which is the signature of a shared hardware limit, not a software inefficiency.

7.3 So what about the 12 ms in Dataset 1?

If the real compute is 2.8 ms, why does Dataset 1 show a 12 ms decode and a device time near 36 ms? Because Dataset 1's run reloads the weights from the host computer on every call. That reload — the Memset and Memcpy events — is a separate, large chunk of memory traffic stacked on top of the 2.8 ms of real compute. In the production run of Dataset 2 you can see it laid bare: the compute kernel is still ~2.85 ms, but the device total balloons to 36.4 ms, of which 33.6 ms is Memset (the weight reload). Leave the weights resident on the device — the way a real model runs — and that reload drops to ~0, leaving the 2.8 ms kernel. §8 walks through this reconciliation step by step.

The compute floor, and why the gap to it is memory, not waste
Put a number on the trickle of math. One token's FFN is 2.013×108 MACs. The AIU's per-core peak is (98.304×1012 FLOPS / 2 / 32) = 1.536×106 MACs/µs/core (from _PEAK_MACS_US_CORE), so across 32 cores the floor is ~4.9×107 MACs/µs. The arithmetic lower bound for a 1-token FFN is therefore
2.013e8 / 4.9e7 ≈ 4 µs.
The measured decode kernel is ~2.8 ms — far above that 4 µs compute floor. But the gap is not wasted overhead, as an earlier draft assumed: the gap is the time to stream 402.7 MB of weights at ~143 GB/s (~2.8 ms). The array could do the math in 4 µs, but it cannot start a multiply until the corresponding weight has arrived, and the weights take 2.8 ms to arrive. Compute floor and memory floor are different yardsticks; at decode, memory is the binding one.

Contrast prefill: M=512 reuses each weight 512×, so now there is real math to do. The compute floor rises to ~2.1 ms — a meaningful fraction of the kernel time — and prefill becomes compute-leaning: the array has enough work that its arithmetic, not just the weight stream, sets the pace. That is exactly why prefill PT-Util is 20–40% (compute is a real share of the kernel) and decode PT-Util is ~0.15% (one token has almost no compute to contribute).

8. The "5–8 ms → 200 ms/token" claim, reconciled

The worry: a model has ~40 layers; 5 ms/MLP × 40 ≈ 200 ms/token, which would be a disaster. Two things are wrong with that multiplication.

8.1 It multiplies a per-call weight reload that a real model pays once

The big decode numbers — 12 ms kernel, ~36 ms device time — are an isolated single-op measurement, and the bulk of them is weight-reload memory traffic, not matmul work. Dataset 2's production run measures exactly where the milliseconds go:

stackkernel compute (ms)device total spyre_ms (ms)weight-reload traffic
torch-spyre (production)2.85236.42Memset 33.57 ms
sendnn (production)2.77536.07Memcpy HtoD 13.59 + DtoH 2.78 + Memset 16.93 = ~33.3 ms
persistent (weights resident)2.83 / 2.80≈ compute~0

Read it plainly: in both stacks the actual matmul is ~2.8 ms, but the device spends ~36 ms because ~33 ms of it is re-fetching the model's weights from the host computer on this one call (Dataset 1 shows the same thing through sendnn's Memcpy HtoD = 10.601 ms, DtoH = 8.487 ms dwarfing the 8.486 ms bmm). Use the analogy from §7: the weights are the tools. A real model leaves its tools on the bench — weights stay resident on the device across every token and every layer, so the reload is paid once at load time, not 40 times per token. The isolated benchmark, by contrast, walks back to the warehouse and re-fetches every tool on every single call. That walk is what the 33 ms Memset/Memcpy is — and it amortizes to ~0 in a real decoder, which is exactly why the persistent-weight kernel is 2.8 ms.

8.2 The latency claim hinges on metric + amortization

Whether "5–8 ms/MLP" is alarming depends entirely on which clock you propagate:

if you multiply…by 40 layersis it real?
spyre_ms production (~36 ms)~1440 msNo — ~33 ms of it is per-call weight reload (Memset/Memcpy) that is paid once, not per layer
Dataset 1 kernel_ms sum (~12 ms)~480 msOverstated — itself inflated by Dataset 1's per-call reload; the real kernel is 2.8 ms
persistent kernel (~2.8 ms)~112 msYes — the real per-layer decode compute, weights resident

The honest per-layer decode quantity is the persistent-weight kernel, ~2.8 ms — weight streaming at the memory ceiling (§7), with no per-call reload and no fixed overhead. The "200 ms/token" figure comes from propagating the least-amortized metric (the isolated spyre_ms), which double-charges a one-time weight load. It is a measurement / amortization artifact, not a hardware ceiling.

The honest one-liner
The real per-layer decode compute is ~2.8 ms — memory-bound weight streaming at the bus ceiling, not idle overhead. The "36 ms" and "200 ms/token" come from an isolated benchmark that reloads ~402 MB of weights from the host on every call (~33 ms of Memset/Memcpy); that reload is ~0 once weights stay resident, which is how a real model runs. It is an amortization question, not a proven hardware ceiling.

9. The LX-frac footnote

One backend knob colors any tsp-vs-sendnn comparison. DXP_LX_FRAC_AVAIL defaults to 0.2: only 20% of the LX scratchpad is offered to the matmul planner, with 80% reserved for the frontend. With less LX, the matmul has to chunk more and torch-spyre looks slower.

  • If that 80% reservation is genuinely idle during these matmuls (it is, in a single-op benchmark with nothing else running), then DXP_LX_FRAC_AVAIL = 1.0 is the fair comparison — and that is what Jamie's table uses (frac=1).
  • Crucially, the planner's split choice — the (m, n, k) it picks — is frac-independent. LX-frac changes how much chunking happens within a chosen split, not which split wins. So the underfill story above is unaffected by the frac setting; only the absolute tsp numbers move.

10. The wide-N matmul: why ~20% slower than sendnn

Separate from the decode story is a prefill flag: the standalone matmul [1, 512, 4096] @ [4096, 12800] — B=1, M=512, K=4096, N=12800 (= 200 sticks) — runs ~1.18–1.22× slower than sendnn in the field. This is the MLP's wide front projection measured in isolation. Two hypotheses: either the cost-model planner is picking a worse (m, n, k) split than it should (fixable in the planner), or the matmul kernel itself is slower regardless of split (a kernel/bandwidth gap). We settled it on device by forcing every legal split and timing each.

10.1 Why this matmul is bandwidth-bound, not compute-bound

Look at what moves. The weight is 4096 × 12800 × 2 B = 100 MB, which is ~86% of all bytes the kernel touches (the M=512 activation in/out are ~4 MB + ~13 MB). The arithmetic intensity (~5.4×1010 FLOPs over ~117 MB ≈ ~460 FLOP/byte) sits right at the AIU's machine balance, so the matmul is pinned against the HBM roofline knee: the floor is set by streaming that 100 MB weight through HBM (~204.8 GB/s → a ~0.6 ms bandwidth floor), not by array arithmetic. The decisive consequence: every split moves the same 100 MB weight. Work-division reshuffles which core does which output column, but the weight stream from HBM is invariant. You cannot divide your way out of a bandwidth wall.

Two findings, one verdict
(1) No split spread. Every legal full-core split — pure-N (8,4), pure-M (32,1), and everything between — lands within ~2% of each other (run-to-run noise). (4,8) is not meaningfully faster than (8,4). The planner's tie-break between cohorts is irrelevant to throughput on this shape.

(2) The whole band sits ~25% over sendnn. Best tsp matmul kernel ≈ 1.18–1.20 ms vs sendnn's 0.962 ms → ~1.25×, matching the field slowdown. Even the fastest forced split stays ~20–25% above sendnn.

Verdict: it is a kernel/bandwidth gap, not a work-division problem. The slowdown lives in the matmul kernel itself (how well each stack streams the wide-N weight from HBM), exactly as the roofline argument in §10.1 predicts. The planner is choosing correctly; there is no split-choice win to capture here.

This is a useful negative result. An earlier paper analysis had hypothesized that ~half the gap was split choice — that (4,8) would beat the planner's pick because the cohort penalty is blind to weight rebroadcast. The device experiment refutes that: on hardware the splits are indistinguishable. The right place to chase the remaining 25% is the matmul kernel's weight-streaming codegen (a deeptools / kernel concern), not the inductor work-division planner.

A premise that didn't hold
On the worktree this ran against, the planner's default split for this shape is pure M=32, not the (8,4,1) an earlier write-up assumed (the two were built from different revisions). It does not change the verdict — (8,4,1) and (4,8,1) were forced explicitly and are no faster than the M=32 default — but it is worth flagging that "the planner picks (8,4,1) here" was itself incorrect.

What the A/B says in one line
The cost-model planner's value shows up at prefill, where M fills the array and the split actually matters: it roughly halves the heuristic's kernel time on the wide projections and reaches or beats sendnn on the square ones. Where it doesn't close the gap (mlp_prefill, 1.20×) the cause is the bandwidth wall of §10, not the split; where it can't move much (decode) the cause is the underfilled array of §6–7, not the split. Every residual traces to a kernel/hardware limit the planner is not responsible for.

12. Restickifies, and the real reason sendnn wins the MLP

A natural question: is torch-spyre slower on the MLP because it pays restickifies (cross-core relayouts) that sendnn avoids? The answer is no — neither stack has a restickify problem on this graph.

12.1 The MLP compiles to zero restickifies

Run through the real torch-spyre passes, the natural fused MLP (up=x@Wu; gate=x@Wg; h=up·silu(gate); out=h@Wd) produces 0 restickifies. The structurally suspicious edge is the [512,16384] intermediate h feeding h@Wd: the 16384 axis is a free/N dimension when the front kernel writes it, then becomes the contraction/K dimension for the down matmul. That N→K flip is exactly the kind of reorientation that could force a restickify — but it doesn't here; the front-matmul output orientation is already compatible with the down-matmul's contraction. Restickifies only appear when the graph contains an explicit transpose/join, and even then they are graph-input/weight layout boundaries, never producer→consumer.

Confirmed empirically at the exact 4096/16384 shape. Compiling the benchmark MLP and reading the pre-scheduling dump: the optimizer emits restickify plan: (none), and the whole compiled op set is exactly 8 ops — 3 batchmatmul (gate, up, down) plus 5 pointwise (neg, exp, add, realdiv, mul: the SiLU/sigmoid decomposition and the gate×up multiply). Zero restickify, stickify, reorient, or transpose ops, of either source kind. The down matmul consumes the [512,16384] intermediate's existing out axis directly as its in=16384 contraction operand — no reorientation inserted.

12.2 What the 2-kernel split actually costs: a 16.78 MB HBM round-trip

torch-spyre lowers the FFN to two kernels (front + down); sendnn fuses it to one. The real cost of the split is that the [512,16384] intermediate h (= 16.78 MB fp16) must be handed between them through HBM. This is not a heuristic guess — memory_planning assigns h to the HBM INTERMEDIATES_SEGMENT; it would only live in LX scratchpad under LX_PLANNING=1, which the benchmark does not set. So the front kernel writes 16.78 MB to HBM and the down kernel reads it back. sendnn's single fused bmm keeps h tiled on-chip and never spills it.

The compile dump makes this concrete: the intermediate is buf7, size=16777216 bytes (= 512×16384×2), carrying allocation=[pool=16777216] — a byte offset into the HBM intermediates segment (INTERMEDIATES_SEGMENT = 0x0), not an LX address. (Weights, by contrast, carry allocation=[hbm=…] at 16 GiB+ absolute addresses — a different HBM region.) So the HBM round-trip of the 16.78 MB intermediate is not inferred, it is in the allocation plan.

But the round-trip is small — utilization is the real gap
Quantify it: 2×16.78 MB / 204.8 GB/s ≈ 0.16 ms — only ~4% of the 3.76 ms prefill gap (tsp 9.53 vs sendnn 5.77 ms). The dominant factor is array utilization: sendnn's one fused kernel runs the systolic array at ~39% PT-Util, torch-spyre's two kernels at 21–31%. Both stream roughly the same ~400 MB of weights; sendnn just fills the array ~1.5–1.8× better and pays one launch/setup instead of two.

So "do they not have this?" — correct, but the "this" isn't restickifies (both ~0). sendnn wins by fusing the whole FFN into one kernel, which (a) keeps the 16.78 MB intermediate on-chip instead of round-tripping HBM, and (b) more importantly, schedules the array far better (~39% vs ~25% PT-Util) with a single launch.

13. Roofline: how close are we to the ideal?

Using torch-spyre's own cost-model constants — peak 98.3 TFLOPS DL16 (_PEAK_MACS_US_CORE = 1.536×106 MACs/µs/core × 32 cores) and HBM 204.8 GB/s — the machine balance (roofline knee) is 480 FLOP/byte. That lets us put an ideal floor under each regime and grade both stacks against it.

13.1 Prefill is compute-bound; decode is memory-bound

  • Prefill (M=512): arithmetic intensity ≈ 482 FLOP/byte — right on the knee. Compute floor = 1.03×1011 MACs / 98.3 TFLOPS = 2.10 ms (the HBM floor, 2.09 ms, nearly coincides). Ideal ≈ 2.10 ms.
  • Decode (M=4): arithmetic intensity ≈ 4 FLOP/byte — 120× below the knee, deeply memory-bound. The compute floor is a trivial ~16µs; the binding cost is reading the 402.7 MB of weights: 402.7 MB / 204.8 GB/s = 1.97 ms. Ideal ≈ 1.97 ms.

14. Off the roofline: what's the gap really made of?

This section rebuilds the whole story from the ground up, in plain language. We will start with the single question every chip faces, measure the one number that actually decides it (how fast the device can move data), and then re-read prefill and decode in that light. No prior accelerator knowledge is assumed; each idea is introduced before it is used.

14.1 The chip has two jobs. Which one is the bottleneck?

Every time the accelerator runs one of these matmuls, it is really doing two things at once:

  1. Fetch the weights from memory. The model's weight matrices live in the device's main memory (called HBM — just "the big memory on the chip"). The math cannot start until the relevant weights have travelled from that memory to the compute engine. Think of HBM as a warehouse and the weights as tools you have to carry to the workbench.
  2. Do the math. Multiply the incoming token(s) by those weights. This happens on the PT array — an 8×8 grid of 64 little multiply cells. Picture it as an assembly line that only runs at full speed when tokens keep streaming in to keep every cell fed (this is the systolic array from §6).

The path between the warehouse and the assembly line is a bus — literally a set of wires with a fixed top speed, like a highway with a fixed number of lanes. No matter how clever the software is, you cannot push more bytes per second down that highway than its lanes allow. That top speed is called bandwidth, measured in gigabytes per second (GB/s).

So for any given matmul there is a race: does the assembly line finish the math first (then it waits for more weights to arrive — we say it is memory-bound: the chip is faster at the math than memory is at delivering the weights), or does it run out of road on the math before the weights even arrive (compute-bound)? Everything below is figuring out, for prefill and for decode, which side of that race we are on — and the answer turns entirely on the real speed of the highway.

14.2 How fast is the highway, really?

The cost model on paper assumes the highway runs at 204.8 GB/s — the LPDDR5 memory's rated spec. But a spec is a sticker number; what matters is what the silicon actually delivers. So we measured it directly with the simplest possible test: a tiny program, y = x × 2, that reads a block of memory and writes it back out — one read, one write, no math to confuse the picture. We ran it at many block sizes and computed the achieved GB/s for each. Figure 6 is the result.

The takeaway in one sentence: the real highway runs at roughly half its sticker speed — ~113 GB/s when you both read and write, climbing to ~143 GB/s when you mostly read (which is what weight streaming does). Hold onto those two numbers; the rest of this section uses them to re-grade everything.

14.3 Decode, re-told: streaming weights at the memory ceiling

Now apply this to decode. Recall from §7 the two measurements we have to reconcile: Dataset 2 says the real decode compute kernel is ~2.8 ms, while Dataset 1 (and the production run) shows ~12 ms / 36 ms. Which is right? Both are — they measure different things, and Figure 6 tells us how to read them.

Take the 2.8 ms first. A single token reuses each weight exactly once, so the FFN must drag all 402.7 MB of its weight matrices across the highway. At the read-dominated ceiling of ~143 GB/s that is 402.7 MB / 143 GB/s ≈ 2.8 ms — which is exactly the measured kernel time. The kernel is not idle and it is not doing real arithmetic; it is streaming weights at the bus's top read speed, with essentially zero fixed overhead left over. Decode is memory-bound, and torch-spyre and sendnn both sit right on the ceiling (140 and 144 GB/s respectively).

So where do the big 12 ms and 36 ms come from? From reloading the weights from the host computer on every benchmark call — the Memset/Memcpy traffic. Figure 7 lays the whole decode call out on a single timeline.

14.4 Prefill, re-told: compute-leaning, but the compute runs well under peak

Prefill (M=512) processes 512 tokens at once, so each weight is reused 512 times — now there is real math to do, and the assembly line has enough work to lean on the compute side rather than pure weight streaming. But "compute-leaning" does not mean "near the compute peak." Two effects keep it far from the 98.3-TFLOPS sticker:

  • The highway is still half-speed. Streaming the 402.7 MB of weights at the measured ~113 GB/s costs 402.7 / 113 ≈ 3.5 ms — which actually exceeds the 2.10 ms the math would take. So even at prefill, memory is a comparable cost to compute, not a negligible one.
  • Compute and memory run with little overlap. When the two phases do not overlap, the kernel time tends toward their sum, not the larger of the two.
The number that dissolves the "sendnn is 2.75× off peak" puzzle
compute 2.10 ms + weight stream (@~113 GB/s) ~3.5 ms ≈ 5.6 ms  vs  sendnn measured 5.77 ms (Dataset 1) and 7.39 ms persistent (Dataset 2) — the lower figure is within ~3% of the sum. So sendnn's apparent "2.75× off the compute peak" is not wasted compute: it is (a) the real bus being ~half of spec, and (b) compute and memory not overlapping. Measured against the real silicon, sendnn's prefill is essentially at the floor. We keep both datasets' sendnn numbers (5.77 and 7.39 ms) rather than pick one: they bracket the run-to-run and configuration spread, and both are consistent with a memory-leaning kernel near the real floor.

14.5 Why torch-spyre pays a SiLU tax that sendnn doesn't

So far the limits (half-speed bus, no overlap) hit both stacks equally. The one place torch-spyre falls clearly behind sendnn on prefill is a specific, measurable, fixable thing: how each stack handles the SiLU activation.

Plain background first. The matmul runs on the PT array (the 8×8 assembly line). SiLU is not a matmul — it is a per-element curve applied to every number in the wide intermediate — and it runs on a different piece of hardware, the SFP unit (think of it as a second specialist worker sitting next to the assembly line, idle while the matmul runs). The question is whether that second worker does the SiLU at the same time as the matmul (hidden, free) or after it finishes (serial, you pay for it). We measured it on prefill (M=512):

The marginal cost of adding SiLU (1.85 ms) is almost exactly the cost of running SiLU all by itself (1.62 ms) — a ratio of 1.15. That is the fingerprint of serialization: torch-spyre runs SiLU as a separate pass on the SFP worker after the matmul finishes, so its cost simply adds on top. By contrast, in a head-to-head on the full FFN, sendnn's marginal SiLU cost is ~0 (measured −0.07 ms) — sendnn folds SiLU into the matmul's epilogue (the finishing step of the same kernel), so the SFP worker does it concurrently and it disappears into time the matmul was spending anyway. 14.6 Reading "PT-Util 21%" correctly

One last piece of plain-language cleanup, because the PT-Util number is easy to misread. Back in §6 we said PT-Util is "how full the array is." That is the right intuition at decode (one token, almost nothing to compute, so the array genuinely has nothing to do). But at prefill the same number means something different, and the difference matters.

The cost model has a separate quantity, pt_eff (a compute de-rating factor), which is ~1.0 at the split the planner actually chooses ((8,4)/(4,8), which packs 64 M-rows per core — a full array). In other words, the array is being filled. The reported "PT-Util 21–39%" is a different ratio: it is compute_floor / kernel_time, and at prefill the denominator is dominated by the memory term (the ~3.5 ms weight stream that does not overlap the compute).

"PT-Util 21%" does not mean the array is 21% full
It means the array is essentially full but spends most of the kernel waiting on memory — the few-lane highway delivering weights at half-spec. Concretely: with compute ~2.10 ms and a non-overlapped memory stream ~3.5 ms, the arithmetic can occupy at most 2.10 / (2.10 + 3.5) ≈ 37% of the kernel — which is exactly the ~38.7% sendnn reports. The low PT-Util at prefill is the memory wall showing through, not an underfilled array. (At decode it really is "almost no compute," as §6–7 said.)

14.7 So where is torch-spyre's real, addressable headroom?

Putting the whole section together in plain terms: most of the distance from the paper "peak" is hardware reality that no software can erase — the highway runs at ~half its spec, and compute and memory do not overlap. sendnn already pays those costs and sits essentially at the real floor on both prefill and decode. So the question "are torch-spyre's matmul kernels failing to use the hardware?" is, mostly, no — the array is full (pt_eff ≈ 1.0), not fragmented. The genuinely addressable gap is the delta to sendnn, and it lives in two specific, named places:

15. Takeaways

  1. The op is one SwiGLU FFN block: gate & up project 4096→16384, SiLU-gate times up, down projects 16384→4096. Three matmuls, ~2×108 MACs per token.
  2. It lowers to 2 tsp kernels (front fused: gate+up+silu+mul; down) vs 1 sendnn bmm. Sum the two tsp rows to compare.
  3. Jamie's table is kernel time ("bmm kernel time only") — not wall, not spyre_ms.
  4. Decode is not anomalous, it is memory-bound weight streaming. At M=1 the 8×8 array has almost no math to do; PT-Util collapses from ~25% to ~0.15% — not idle bookkeeping but a lack of arithmetic. The real per-layer decode kernel (Dataset 2, weights resident) is ~2.8 ms, which is exactly the FFN's 402.7 MB of weights streamed at the bus's real ~143 GB/s read ceiling, with ~zero fixed overhead. Both stacks (tsp 2.83, sendnn 2.80 ms) sit on that ceiling.
  5. "200 ms/token" is a metric artifact. The big decode numbers (12 ms / 36 ms) are an isolated benchmark reloading all 402.7 MB of weights from the host every call (~33 ms of Memset/Memcpy; in Dataset 1 sendnn's HtoD 10.6 ms alone exceeds the bmm). Production kernel_ms (~2.85 ms) matches persistent compute (~2.83 ms) to within noise — the kernel never stalls on weight arrival. Leave weights resident and the reload is ~0; the right per-layer quantity is ~2.8 ms.
  6. The real lever for decode is filling the idle array: K-split (split the K=16384 reduction across the otherwise-idle cores) is the only way to put work on more than ~1 row at M=1 — which is exactly where the cost-model's split-K decode path applies.
  7. LX-frac=1 is the fair comparison for an isolated benchmark; the split choice is frac-independent.
  8. The wide-N ~20% slowdown is a kernel/bandwidth gap, not a split problem (§10). Forcing every legal split of [1,512,4096]@[4096,12800] on device gives a ~2%-wide band all ~25% over sendnn; the matmul is HBM-bound streaming a 100 MB weight (~86% of bytes), so no split helps. Chase it in the matmul kernel's weight streaming, not in work division.
  9. The planner's value is concentrated at prefill (§11). Across the six matmul shapes it roughly halves the heuristic's kernel time on wide prefill projections (qo 2.59×, mlp 2.07×) and reaches/beats sendnn on the square ones; decode shapes barely move because the array is underfilled regardless of split. Every place it trails sendnn traces to §10 (bandwidth) or §6–7 (underfill), not to a wrong split.
  10. The MLP has 0 restickifies (§12, confirmed in the compile dump) — the slowdown vs sendnn is not a relayout problem. The 2-kernel split round-trips the 16.78 MB intermediate through HBM (~0.16 ms, minor); the real gap is array utilization (~25% PT-Util vs sendnn's ~39%) from emitting two under-filled kernels instead of one fused bmm.
  11. Distance from the roofline (§13): graded against the 204.8 GB/s spec, both regimes look 4–6× off — but that floor uses a bandwidth the silicon does not deliver. Re-graded against the measured bus (§14), prefill is memory-leaning (sendnn near the real floor) and the persistent decode kernel sits right on the real ~143 GB/s read ceiling. Decode is memory-bound at the ceiling, not overhead-stuck below its roofline.
  12. Off the roofline, decomposed (§14): the measured bus is ~55% of spec (~113 GB/s balanced copy, ~143 GB/s read-dominated streaming) — it does NOT collapse at decode. At real BW, prefill is memory-leaning and sendnn is near-optimal (compute 2.10 + weight stream ~3.5 = ~5.6 ms ≈ measured 5.77). torch-spyre's one clearly addressable prefill loss is SiLU serialized after the matmul (measured marginal +1.85 ms vs sendnn's ~0, which folds SiLU into the matmul epilogue): emit SiLU as an epilogue to put ~1.7 ms back. On decode both stacks are already at the memory ceiling (~2.8 ms); the only lever is fusing the two kernels and keeping weights resident, not faster memory.
